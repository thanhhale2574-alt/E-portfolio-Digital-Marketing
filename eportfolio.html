Executive Summary
This e-portfolio analyses and applies key digital marketing frameworks to evaluate how businesses build engagement, optimise customer experience, and improve marketing effectiveness in the digital environment. Entry 1 presents the Ladder of Engagement model applied to Starbucks, showing how the brand moves customers from basic ratings to full product co-creation. Entry 2 critiques TikTok using the Zones of Social Media model, assessing strengths and limitations across all four zones with implications for marketers. Entry 3 develops a comprehensive website checklist grounded in the 5Ss framework and applies it to Zara.com, identifying strong UX design but weaknesses in conversion optimisation and personalisation. Entry 4 analyses AI-powered personalisation as a strategic marketing trend, evaluating opportunities, challenges, and how brands across different scales can leverage it through the customer journey.
Entry 01 · Framework Application

The Ladder of Engagement

Applied to Starbucks · Framework: Smith & Zook (2020)
1.1 Theory

What is the Ladder of Engagement?

The Ladder of Engagement is a strategic model that illustrates how customers can drive businesses through different forms of interaction, ranging from low to high levels, with the ultimate goal being co-creation (Smith and Zook, 2020). This model not only focuses on shaping messages, but also aims to influence products, services, processes, and the way a business operates (Chaffey and Smith, 2022).

According to Nielsen's (2006) Participation Inequality rule, in most online communities 90% of users simply observe, 9% participate to a limited extent, and only 1% create the majority of content and activity — meaning businesses must build strategies tailored to each group.

1.2 Application

Applying the Ladder to Starbucks

Starbucks is a prime example of effectively implementing the Ladder of Engagement. By encouraging participation at multiple levels, Starbucks builds emotional connections and views customers as valuable partners, not just shoppers (Business Model Analyst, 2024).

Scope note: This entry focuses on five levels with clear, documented evidence from Starbucks — Ratings & Reviews, Ideas, Ads, Processes, and Products. Levels such as Sharing and Discussions occur organically on social media but lack brand-specific initiatives distinct enough to analyse separately within this scope.
Level 1 · Basic Interaction
Ratings & Reviews
Customers provide ratings and reviews via the Starbucks app on App Store and Google Play, or third-party platforms such as Yelp. These not only inform other customers but provide feedback for Starbucks to improve the customer experience (Rachmiani et al., 2024).
App Store Google Play Yelp
Level 2 · Active Contribution
Ideas
Starbucks launched My Starbucks Idea to allow customers to submit suggestions for new products, service improvements, and store innovations (Lê, 2022). This enabled customers to actively participate in the brand's innovation process. Over 150,000 ideas were submitted.
My Starbucks Idea Platform
Level 3 · Content Co-Creation
Ads — User Generated Content
Starbucks' #RedCupContest campaign encouraged users to photograph signature red cups and share on Instagram. The campaign received over 40,000 entries, demonstrating the power of UGC where customers become brand ambassadors (Zeno Digital Marketing, 2025). According to Smith & Zook (2020), the Ads level refers to customers creating branded content — evidenced precisely by this campaign.
Instagram UGC · 40,000+ entries
Level 4 · Operational Influence
Processes
Facing customer attrition, Starbucks launched the "Back to Starbucks" campaign to restore brand experience (Znews, 2025). This focused on renovating store spaces, enhancing in-store experience, and improving service quality to rebuild emotional connection — changes directly driven by customer feedback (Warnick and Tan, 2025).
Back to Starbucks Campaign
Level 5 · Co-Creation Peak
Products
At the highest level, Starbucks demonstrates full co-creation by implementing customer ideas directly. Of 150,000+ submissions to My Starbucks Idea, 277 ideas were implemented — from new menu items to digital innovations such as mobile payment and free Wi-Fi (Starbucks Melody, 2017). This shows customer contributions being realised as actual product outcomes.
277 ideas implemented
Key Takeaway

Starbucks demonstrates the full progression of the Ladder of Engagement — from passive reviews to active co-creation of products. This progression shows that a strategically designed engagement model can transform consumers into brand advocates and generate sustainable value, validating Smith & Zook's (2020) framework in practice.

Entry 02 · Platform Critique

Social Media Platform Evaluation

Platform: TikTok · Framework: Zones of Social Media (Tuten & Solomon, 2018)
2.1 Framework

The Zones of Social Media

Social media refers to online channels that allow individuals to communicate, from small groups to millions of people (Tuten and Solomon, 2018). To facilitate comparison and management of social media spaces, Tuten and Solomon (2018) classify them into four zones based on the primary function of each platform.

🤝

Social Community

Building and maintaining relationships through multi-directional interaction, discussions, and collaboration among people with shared interests.

📢

Social Publishing

Content production and distribution, allowing users to create and share knowledge without traditional publishing constraints.

🎭

Social Entertainment

Entertainment-oriented activities and content designed to provide enjoyment and pleasure for users.

🛒

Social Commerce

Use of social media in buying and selling, where users influence purchasing decisions through reviews and social interactions.

2.2 Critique

TikTok Evaluated Across All Four Zones

TikTok is a social media application allowing users to create, watch, and share short-form videos with distinctive music and sound effects (Investopedia, 2025). With over 5 billion downloads, it is one of the fastest-growing platforms globally (Business of Apps, 2026). Marketers use TikTok for organic interaction, influencer collaborations, hashtag challenges, and conversion through TikTok Shop (Admetrics, 2026; Sprout Social, 2026; Brands Vietnam, 2025b).

🤝
Social Community
Moderate strength

TikTok is strong in forming interest-based communities like #BookTok and #Fashion through features such as Duet, Stitch, and livestream, creating a sense of belonging (JAYbranding, 2025; VietNamNet News, 2024).

Limitation: Unlike Facebook Groups or LinkedIn — where communities have clear structure, forums, and sustained dialogue — TikTok interactions revolve around short-term viral content rather than lasting relationships, making community maintenance difficult (Tuten and Solomon, 2018).

Implication for marketers: TikTok should not be the primary channel for nurturing loyal communities — it is better suited as an entry point for attracting new audiences.

📢
Social Publishing
Strong

TikTok's "discovery-first" For You Page allows content to reach wide audiences regardless of follower count, giving both large and small brands equal viral opportunity (Baumann et al., 2026). This is a distinct advantage over Instagram or YouTube, where reach is largely tied to follower count or ad spend.

Limitation: Short content lifecycle and remix/stitch features can alter brand messages (InfluenceFlow, 2025).

Implication: Marketers need flexible brand guidelines suited to platform culture, rather than enforcing rigid content formats.

🎭
Social Entertainment
Dominant zone

This is TikTok's core competitive zone. Its personalised algorithm continuously suggests relevant short videos, creating an immersive environment and increasing session time (Kaye et al., 2020). The platform's strength here clearly differentiates it from Facebook (Community), LinkedIn (Publishing), or Shopee (Commerce).

Limitation: Brand content only succeeds if produced in TikTok's entertainment language — overly promotional content is immediately ignored. Trend-dependence requires continuous production schedules and rapid response, creating significant resource pressure (Montag et al., 2021).

Implication: Marketers must commit to "native" content production — treating TikTok like a media channel, not a broadcast tool.

🛒
Social Commerce
Rapidly growing

TikTok integrates content and commerce through TikTok Shop and shoppable livestreams, enabling the full customer journey — discovery to purchase — within the app (Putri et al., 2024). Compared to Shopee or Lazada, TikTok's advantage is stimulating impulse buying through entertaining content, converting viewers without them actively searching.

Limitation: Consumer trust in quality and security remains a barrier, making TikTok Shop most suitable for low-priced, quick-decision products — not high-consideration categories like electronics or financial products (Putri et al., 2024).

Implication: Marketers should match product category carefully to TikTok commerce — impulse and lifestyle products convert well; considered purchases do not.

Overall Judgement

Through the Zones of Social Media lens, TikTok operates most powerfully in Social Entertainment — this is its core competitive differentiator. The most effective strategy for marketers is to lead with Entertainment, then extend into Publishing and Commerce, rather than attempting to use TikTok as a primary Community or branding channel. TikTok is best suited to brands seeking to reach new audiences through entertaining content, but must be combined with other platforms to nurture long-term loyalty (Tuten and Solomon, 2018).

Entry 03 · Website Evaluation

Website Checklist

Applied to Zara.com · Framework: 5Ss (Chaffey & Smith, 2022)
3.1 Framework

Building an Effective Website Checklist

In the digital age, websites are a crucial owned media channel for sales, branding, and customer interaction. To be effective, websites must deliver value and support users to increase conversions and build long-term relationships (Chaffey and Smith, 2022).

The checklist is grounded in the 5Ss framework by Chaffey and Smith (2022) — Sell, Serve, Speak, Save, Sizzle — to ensure every evaluation criterion maps to a specific marketing objective. From a technical perspective, page speed, Core Web Vitals, security, and stable performance are critical (Jain, 2023). Visibility and conversion require basic SEO practices, strong UX/UI, and clear navigation (Xinghai, 2023).

Sell

Convert & Sell

Drive transactions through optimised conversion paths, clear CTAs, and checkout design.

Serve

Serve Customers

Deliver useful content, fast performance, mobile responsiveness, and security.

Speak

Speak to Customers

Personalise communication and recommendations based on user behaviour and context.

Save

Save Costs

Leverage SEO and organic search to reduce dependence on paid acquisition.

Sizzle

Create Excitement

Build brand appeal through distinctive visual design and emotional experience.

Each checklist criterion below is mapped to one of the 5Ss to ensure strategic alignment with marketing objectives.

Zara.com excels in visual design (4.5/5) — its minimalist language and high-quality imagery create a premium shopping experience consistent with the brand's fast-fashion aesthetic positioning. Checkout, product information, and mobile responsiveness are equally strong, reflecting a well-executed user experience strategy (Chaffey and Smith, 2022).

Key Weakness

Conversion Optimisation: 3.5/5

Unlike H&M or ASOS — which use countdown timers and "Only X left" alerts to create scarcity — Zara applies almost no urgency triggers. These techniques fall under the Sell dimension of the 5Ss (Chaffey and Smith, 2022) and can significantly improve conversion rates. Similarly, Personalisation (4/5) lags behind Amazon or Zalando's behaviour-driven recommendation systems.

Entry 04 · Trend Analysis

AI-Powered Personalisation

Evaluating the trend from a marketer's perspective
4.1 Trend Overview

What is AI-Powered Personalisation?

AI-powered personalisation is the use of artificial intelligence — including Machine Learning, recommendation systems, and predictive analytics — to exploit data and create tailored experiences for each individual user (Singhal et al., 2025). By processing behavioural data, purchase history, and real-time signals, AI builds highly personalised interactions to increase engagement and customer connection. This integrates technologies such as ML, Natural Language Processing, and Generative AI to identify behavioural patterns (Hayes and Downie, 2024).

4.2 Marketer's Perspective

Evaluating the Trend as a Marketer

From a marketer's perspective, AI-powered personalisation fundamentally changes how campaigns are planned, executed, and measured. Instead of building one message for the entire market, marketers can now segment audiences at the individual level — a "segment of one" — based on real behavioural data rather than demographic assumptions (Chaffey and Ellis-Chadwick, 2022).

This shift requires a different KPI framework. Beyond reach and impressions, effective measurement of AI personalisation requires tracking: Click-Through Rate (CTR) per personalised segment, Customer Lifetime Value (CLV) for long-term loyalty impact, Churn Rate for retention effectiveness, and Revenue Per User (RPU) for direct conversion impact (Hardcastle et al., 2025).

Opportunity

Improved Campaign Performance

Personalised content shortens the purchase decision process and increases relevance, improving conversion rates by up to 25% (Marketing Insider, 2026). Marketers also achieve ROI improvements of 20–30% through more accurate budget allocation and reduced wasted spend on irrelevant campaigns (Futurism Technologies, 2026).

Opportunity

Customer Loyalty & Satisfaction

Personalised experiences make customers feel understood, strengthening satisfaction and loyalty. This drives a shift from mass advertising to tailored messaging, contributing to sustained engagement — particularly in developing markets where e-commerce growth generates large volumes of behavioural data (An and Ngo, 2025).

4.3 How Brands Can Take Advantage

Strategic Application Across the Customer Journey

Brands can leverage AI-powered personalisation most effectively by mapping it to each stage of the customer journey, rather than applying it only at the point of purchase.

Awareness
Predictive targeting & lookalike modelling to reach audiences most likely to convert, based on existing customer data
Consideration
Dynamic website content that adapts homepage and product highlights based on each user's browsing history in real time
Conversion
Personalised cart abandonment emails with tailored offers based on previous order value and product interest signals
Retention
Churn prediction models to proactively engage at-risk customers with personalised content before they disengage (Hardcastle et al., 2025)
Large Brands

Amazon & Netflix Model

Netflix uses data-driven algorithms, A/B testing, and multi-variable recommendation systems to create fully personalised homepages. Amazon applies deep learning to predict needs and enhance product discovery (Khandelwal, 2019). Critically, both personalise the entire customer journey — not just product recommendations, but search results, emails, and post-purchase touchpoints.

SMEs

Accessible Third-Party Tools

Brands without proprietary AI infrastructure can still capitalise through: Klaviyo for email personalisation, Dynamic Yield for web personalisation, and Meta Advantage+ for automatically optimised advertising. These deliver meaningful personalisation at significantly lower cost than building bespoke AI systems (Chaffey and Smith, 2022).

4.4 Limitations & Risks

Critical Challenges Marketers Must Address

Risk 01

Data Privacy & GDPR

Large-scale personal data collection raises serious ethical and legal concerns, particularly under GDPR in Europe and increasingly stringent global data protection regulations. Marketers must ensure transparency and explicit consent in all data collection practices (Hardcastle et al., 2025).

Risk 02

Filter Bubble Effect

Personalisation algorithms can trap users in echo chambers, exposing them only to content matching existing preferences. This limits product discovery and may reduce the richness of the shopping experience over time, ultimately weakening engagement (Pariser, 2011).

Risk 03

Infrastructure Cost

Building proprietary AI personalisation infrastructure requires significant investment, making it primarily accessible to large enterprises. SMEs must rely on third-party tools or risk under-investing in personalisation relative to competitors (An and Ngo, 2025).

Looking Ahead

AI-powered personalisation will continue to evolve with the integration of Generative AI — enabling fully personalised marketing content to be created in real time for each individual, rather than just recommending existing products. This represents the next frontier for marketers, demanding that brands begin building robust data infrastructure now to remain competitive. Critically, the brands that will win are those who balance personalisation effectiveness with genuine data transparency — maintaining consumer trust as the foundation of long-term engagement.
